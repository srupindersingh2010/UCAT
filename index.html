Section Strategies
Tailored to Aman's profile — gaming brain, physics strength, ward experience
Verbal Reasoning
44 questions · 21 mins · 28s per question
Answer only from the text — never use outside knowledge. Read the question before the passage. Max 2 minutes per passage then move on regardless. Master the Can't Tell option — this is where most marks are lost.
⚠ Needs most work — start earliest and read NHS/science news daily to build speed naturally.
Decision Making
29 questions · 31 mins · 64s per question
Learn all 6 question types separately: syllogisms, Venn diagrams, probability, logical puzzles, assumptions, interpreting information. Always draw diagrams on paper — never do it mentally. Most time per question of any section.
✓ Gaming brain = natural logical instinct. Should be Aman's highest scoring section.
Quantitative Reasoning
36 questions · 26 mins · 43s per question
Physics and Chemistry maths is the core. Percentages, ratios, speed/distance/time, area/volume, compound interest. Practise using the onscreen calculator efficiently. Mental arithmetic for simple steps saves critical seconds.
✓ Strong natural advantage. Focus on speed — accuracy will come quickly with the science background.
Situational Judgement
69 questions · 26 mins · 22s per question
Read GMC Good Medical Practice. Priority order always: patient safety → honesty → escalation → teamwork. Partial marks for near-correct answers. Must hit Band 1 or 2 — many schools auto-reject Band 4. No negative marking, always answer.
★ Ward volunteering is a massive edge. Aman has lived these scenarios. Reflect on real ward moments before each SJT session.

Critical Rules
Break none of these.

Never skip a mock review

Doing mocks without reviewing wrong answers is the single biggest mistake UCAT candidates make. The review session is where the actual learning happens. Always spend at least 1 hour reviewing every mock immediately after.

Never leave a blank answer

There is NO negative marking in the UCAT. Always answer every single question. Flag and return if time allows but never, ever leave a blank. Every unanswered question is a free mark given away.

No new techniques in the final 2 weeks

New strategies in Weeks 10 and 11 cause confusion, not improvement. Only use techniques that are already automatic and familiar. Trust what has been built over the previous 9 weeks.

Ignore Abstract Reasoning resources

Abstract Reasoning was removed from the UCAT in 2025. Any book, app, or website still listing it as a section is out of date. Time spent on it is completely wasted.

Book test slot in Week 1

Booking opens 23 June 2026. Aim for 10–12 September. Coventry and Birmingham Pearson VUE centres fill weeks in advance. Book immediately when booking opens — do not delay.

Apply for the UCAT bursary

The bursary covers the £70 test fee for eligible students. Check eligibility at ucat.ac.uk and apply before booking. Coventry school background may qualify.

Rest is preparation

Gym and martial arts sessions are not wasted UCAT time. Physical activity measurably improves cognitive performance. Keep training throughout all 11 weeks. Rest days are scheduled deliberately.

Keep a score tracker every week

Log every mock score in the Score Tracker tab. Seeing an upward trend week on week is one of the most powerful motivators available. Aman knows what levelling up feels like.

For Aman — When It Gets Hard
Read this on the bad days. Not the good ones.

When a mock score disappoints

Every wrong answer in practice is a point saved on test day. In martial arts you don't quit because a sparring session went badly — you find out what needs work and come back sharper. The UCAT is exactly the same. A bad mock is not a bad sign. It is information.

When time pressure feels impossible

Aman has built Lego at speed. He makes decisions in complex games under pressure. His brain is already wired for fast pattern recognition and rapid spatial thinking. The UCAT is a new game — and every game has strategies that can be learned. He has been learning those strategies for weeks.

When motivation drops mid-prep

Picture a patient on that ward. Someone who was frightened, lonely, and needed someone to bring them food and stop to have a conversation. That is who Aman will one day treat, diagnose, and help to recover. The UCAT is 11 weeks. Medicine is a lifetime. The patients need him to get through this.

What Aman already is

He is 17 years old. He volunteers in an NHS ward with real patients. He is earning DofE Gold — one of the most demanding youth awards in existence. He trains in martial arts. He goes to the gym. He is disciplined, compassionate, and resilient. He is already more prepared for medicine than most applicants twice his age.

On the morning of test day

Everything that was built over 11 weeks is inside Aman's brain on that morning. The techniques are trained. The strategies are automatic. The scenarios feel familiar because he has lived them in that ward. Eat a proper breakfast. Leave 45 minutes early. Arrive calm. The test is two hours. He is ready.
